Antimicrobial resistance is a critical global health threat, necessitating the development of novel strategies to target high-priority pathogens such as *Pseudomonas aeruginosa*. The lipopolysaccharide (LPS) transport machinery is a validated target for the antimicrobial peptide thanatin; however, the structural determinants governing its disruption of the periplasmic LptH and the outer-membrane LptD transport proteins remain poorly defined. This study employs a dual framework, utilizing *in silico* modelling and native mass spectrometry (nMS) dimer disruption assay to evaluate non-canonical thanatin orthologs and a novel fragment-derived peptide.

While previous studies relied on monomer binding assays, this work demonstrates that specific orthologs achieve strong LptH dimer disruption, highlighting residues I9, Y10, and R14 as likely molecular drivers of this activity. Furthermore, *in silico* analysis investigated a previously unexplored interaction between the non-canonical thanatins and LptD, predicting residues I9, M21, and I21 to be critical for disrupting LptD interactions with LptH. Although the novel fragment peptide exhibited lower disruption than the canonical scaffold, it provides a method and structural backbone for future optimization. These findings offer a framework for the rational design of thanatin analogs to combat resistant *P. aeruginosa*, as current peptides show lower antimicrobial efficacy against this pathogen compared to less clinically relevant species like *E. coli*.

This repository contains the generalized R scripts used to process binding energy data and generate the figures presented in the dissertation. The code is designed to be modular, allowing users to input their own experimental values to reproduce the analysis or adapt the visualizations for similar protein-protein interaction studies. Additionally, the nMS result files include the original data for users wishing to recreate the nMS spectra figures presented in the dissertation.
